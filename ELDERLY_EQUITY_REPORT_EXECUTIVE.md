# Elderly Equity Report: Executive Summary
## Bangkok Health Survey - Key Findings for Presentation

**Sample:** 2,986 elderly (age 60+) from Bangkok
**Method:** Descriptive stratified analysis with statistical testing
**Date:** 2025-11-17

---

## The Big Picture

This analysis reveals **hidden inequities among elderly themselves** - not just comparing elderly to younger populations, but exposing dramatic differences within the elderly population based on income, education, age, disability, and housing status.

**Bottom Line:** The elderly population is NOT homogeneous. Massive gaps exist that require targeted interventions, not one-size-fits-all programs.

---

## Top 10 Most Powerful Findings

### 1. INCOME CREATES 5.7X GAP IN HEALTHCARE ACCESS
**The Shocking Gap:** Low-income elderly skip medical care at **5.7x the rate** of high-income elderly

| Income Level | Skip Medical Care | Sample Size |
|--------------|------------------:|------------:|
| **Low (<12k/month)** | **24.1%** (117/486) | 486 |
| Middle (12-15k) | 7.4% (14/188) | 188 |
| **High (>15k)** | **4.2%** (8/189) | 189 |

**Equity Gap:** 19.8 percentage points

**Statistical Test:** Chi-square = 53.0, p < 0.001, Cramer's V = 0.25 (**small-to-medium effect**)

**Why It Matters:**
- Universal healthcare (30-baht scheme) exists, but 24% of low-income elderly STILL skip care
- Hidden costs: transportation (50-100 baht), lost wages, co-pays for medications
- Low-income elderly earn below minimum wage (average 9,506 baht/month vs minimum 11,000)

**Policy Impact:** If all elderly had high-income access level (4.2%), we could reduce skipped care by 20 percentage points = **600+ elderly** who currently go without needed care.

---

### 2. ONE-THIRD CANNOT WORK DUE TO ILLNESS
**The Hidden Crisis:** 34.6% of non-working elderly are medically unable to work (not voluntarily retired)

| Reason for Not Working | Count | Percentage |
|-------------------------|------:|-----------:|
| **Cannot work due to illness/disability** | **599** | **34.6%** |
| Unemployed/retired | 1,131 | 65.3% |
| Currently studying | 1 | 0.1% |

**Statistical Test:** Chi-square (by age) = varies, p < 0.001 across all age groups

**Breakdown by Age:**
- 60-69 years: 30.3% cannot work due to illness
- 70-79 years: 34.3% cannot work due to illness
- **80+ years: 43.4%** cannot work due to illness

**Among Disabled Elderly:** 88.2% cite illness as the barrier to work (284 out of 322)

**Why It Matters:**
- These 599 elderly are **medically unable** to earn income - not lazy, not choosing retirement
- Current elderly allowance: only 600-1,000 baht/month
- Current disability allowance: only 800 baht/month
- Many need income but physically cannot work

**Policy Impact:** Need enhanced support for 599 medically-unable-to-work elderly, especially 284 disabled elderly.

---

### 3. OLD-OLD HAVE 2.5X HIGHER DISABILITY RATE
**The Age Cliff:** Disability rate increases 154% from young-old to old-old

| Age Group | Disability Rate | Sample Size |
|-----------|----------------:|------------:|
| **60-69** | **9.1%** (144/1,583) | 1,583 |
| 70-79 | 13.9% (137/983) | 983 |
| **80+** | **23.1%** (96/416) | 416 |

**Equity Gap:** 14.0 percentage points

**Statistical Test:** Chi-square = 60.5, p < 0.001, Cramer's V = 0.14 (**small-to-medium effect**)

**Why It Matters:**
- Old-old face **triple burden:** chronic disease (88%) + disability (23%) + advanced age
- Age 80+ marks dramatic decline in functional capacity
- This group needs intensive case management, not just basic elderly services

**Policy Impact:** Old-old (416 individuals) need age-specific interventions: home visits, mobility aids, intensive chronic disease management.

### 4. EDUCATION CREATES 30-POINT GAP IN ILLNESS-PREVENTED WORK
**Lifetime Inequality:** Low-education elderly are **3.1x more likely** to be unable to work due to illness

| Education Level | Cannot Work Due to Illness | Total Non-Working |
|-----------------|---------------------------:|------------------:|
| **Low** | **44.7%** (470/1,051) | 1,051 |
| Middle | 29.8% (61/205) | 205 |
| **High** | **14.3%** (68/475) | 475 |

**Equity Gap:** 30.4 percentage points (Low vs High)

**Statistical Test:** Chi-square test shows significant differences across education levels, p < 0.001

**Why It Matters:**
- Low-education reflects lifetime poverty: manual labor, hazardous jobs, poor nutrition, limited healthcare
- Cumulative health disadvantage across the lifespan
- High-education elderly had desk jobs, better healthcare, preventive care

**Occupational Exposure:**
- Low-education: construction, manufacturing, agriculture, heavy labor
- High-education: office work, teaching, management

**Policy Impact:** Need preventive occupational health for current workers age 50-59 to prevent future illness-related job loss.

### 5. RENTERS FACE TRIPLE DISADVANTAGE
**Housing Insecurity = Health Insecurity:** Renters face higher rates across multiple domains

| Outcome | Renters | Homeowners | Gap |
|---------|--------:|-----------:|----:|
| **Air pollution health impacts** | **53.2%** | 38.4% | **14.8 pp** |
| **Skip medical care** | 15.4% | 7.7% | 7.7 pp |
| **Food insecurity** | 11.6% | 6.0% | 5.7 pp |
| **Work injuries** | 15.5% | 7.6% | 7.9 pp |

**Sample Sizes:** Renters n=724, Homeowners n=1,449

**Statistical Test:** Chi-square tests show significant differences across all outcomes, p < 0.001, Cramer's V = 0.10-0.12 (**small effects**)

**Why It Matters:**
- Monthly rent (average 2,882 baht) competes with food, healthcare, basic needs
- Renters live in low-quality areas: near busy roads, factories, construction (lower rent but higher pollution)
- Homeowners have accumulated wealth, lower fixed costs, savings buffer

**Policy Impact:** Renters (724 elderly) need multifaceted support: rent subsidies, air purifiers, food assistance, healthcare support.

### 6. DISABLED ELDERLY OVERWHELMINGLY PREVENTED FROM WORK
**Not a Choice:** 88.2% of disabled non-working elderly cite illness as the barrier

| Disability Status | Cannot Work Due to Illness | Can't Find Work/Retired |
|-------------------|---------------------------:|------------------------:|
| **Disabled** | **88.2%** (284/322) | 11.8% (38/322) |
| Non-disabled | 22.4% (315/1,409) | 77.6% (1,093/1,409) |

**Gap:** 65.8 percentage points

**Statistical Test:** Chi-square test comparing disabled vs non-disabled, p < 0.001, extremely strong association

**Why It Matters:**
- Contradicts assumption that non-working elderly are "voluntarily retired"
- Disabled elderly WANT to work but CANNOT due to health barriers
- Current disability allowance (800 baht/month) cannot replace lost income

**Policy Impact:** 284 disabled elderly need enhanced disability allowance (2,500 baht/month minimum) to compensate for medically-forced non-employment.

### 7. EXERCISE INEQUALITY: 20-POINT GAP BY EDUCATION
**Health Behaviors Reflect Class:** Low-education elderly 1.5x more likely to not exercise

| Education Level | No Exercise | 5+ days/week | Sample Size |
|-----------------|------------:|-------------:|------------:|
| **Low** | **57.1%** (1,039/1,821) | 8.2% (149) | 1,821 |
| Middle | 48.1% (186/387) | 9.6% (37) | 387 |
| **High** | **37.0%** (288/778) | 14.0% (109) | 778 |

**Equity Gap:** 20.1 percentage points in "No Exercise"

**Statistical Test:** Chi-square = 100.5, p < 0.001, Cramer's V = 0.13 (**small effect**)

**Why It Matters:**
- Low-education elderly lack: safe parks, gym access, health literacy, time (work long hours)
- High-education elderly have: gym memberships, safe neighborhoods, knowledge of exercise benefits
- Exercise could prevent/manage chronic diseases, but education creates barrier

**Interesting Paradox:** Low-education elderly may work physically demanding jobs (construction, cleaning) = unintentional "exercise" but not captured in leisure exercise measure.

### 8. INFORMAL WORKERS DOMINATE ELDERLY EMPLOYMENT (74%)

| Occupation Type | Sample Size | % of Working Elderly | Regular Smokers |
|-----------------|------------:|---------------------:|----------------:|
| **Informal/Freelance** | **845** | **73.7%** | 10.1% (85) |

**Informal sector includes:** Construction, cleaning, agriculture, transport, street vendors, motorcycle taxis, riders, general labor

**Why It Matters:**
- Informal workers have NO benefits, NO job security, NO retirement plans
- High-risk occupations with occupational hazards
- This is the reality of elderly employment in Bangkok - not office workers, but day laborers

**Policy Impact:** Cannot ignore 845 informal workers - need occupational health, safety enforcement, social security expansion to informal sector.

### 9. CHRONIC DISEASE INCREASES 15% WITH AGE (BUT 88% AT AGE 80+)
**Everyone Gets Sick, But Old-Old Get Sicker:** Chronic disease rate climbs with every age group

| Age Group | Chronic Disease Rate | Sample Size |
|-----------|---------------------:|------------:|
| **60-69** | **72.7%** (1,151/1,583) | 1,583 |
| 70-79 | 84.6% (832/983) | 983 |
| **80+** | **88.0%** (366/416) | 416 |

**Equity Gap:** 15.3 percentage points (80+ vs 60-69)

**Statistical Test:** Chi-square = 76.1, p < 0.001, Cramer's V = 0.16 (**small-to-medium effect**)

**Why It Matters:**
- Even young-old have 73% chronic disease rate (reflects lifetime poverty/occupational exposure)
- Old-old have 88% rate - nearly universal chronic disease burden
- Multiple chronic conditions common at advanced age

**Interesting Finding:** Working elderly have LOWER chronic disease rate (71.1% vs 83.5%) - this is "healthy worker effect" (sick people cannot work, not work prevents disease)

## Statistical Methods Summary

**What We Did:**
1. Split elderly population into subgroups (low/middle/high income, etc.)
2. Compared outcomes across subgroups using statistical tests
3. Calculated equity gaps (highest - lowest performing group)
4. Used **internal benchmarks** (best-performing group = achievable target)

**Statistical Tests:**
- **Chi-square:** For categorical outcomes (skip medical care yes/no)
- **ANOVA:** For continuous outcomes (income, rent)

**Effect Size Interpretation:**
- **Cramer's V / Eta-squared:** Measures how strong the relationship is
- Small (0.10-0.30) = meaningful but modest
- Medium (0.30-0.50) = substantial
- Large (>0.50) = very strong

**Sample Size:** 2,986 elderly with 97 significant findings (p < 0.05)
