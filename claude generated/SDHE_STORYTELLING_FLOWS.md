## 1. Informal Workers - "The Education Cascade"

**Population**: 1,330 informal workers (20% of survey)
**Key Characteristic**: No employment contracts, no benefits, precarious work

### The Health Inequality Pattern

Among Bangkok's informal workers, health outcomes follow a clear cascade: **education determines income, income determines healthcare access, and healthcare access determines disease outcomes.** But this population also shows unique protective factors that don't exist for other groups.

---

### Pathway 1: Education as the Master Key (30% of health influence)

**The divide**:
- Primary education only: **53.1% chronic disease**
- Bachelor+ education: **30.1% chronic disease**
- **Gap: 23.0 percentage points** (r=-0.217, p<0.001)

| Education Level | Chronic Disease Rate | Gap |
|----------------|---------------------|-----|
| Primary or less | 53.1% | (baseline) |
| Bachelor+ | 30.1% | -23.0 pp |
| **Effect** | — | r=-0.217, p<0.001 |

**Why education matters so much**:

Education isn't just about knowledge—it **unlocks multiple downstream pathways**:

1. **Income pathway**: Bachelor+ workers earn **25,557 baht/month** vs primary-educated workers at **13,900 baht/month** (84% income gap, 1.84× multiplier)

| Education Level | Monthly Income | Gap |
|----------------|----------------|-----|
| Primary or less | 13,900 baht | (baseline) |
| Bachelor+ | 25,557 baht | +11,657 baht |
| **Multiplier** | **1.84×** | p<0.001 |

2. **Health behavior pathway**: Educated workers exercise regularly at **29.2%** vs **15.7%** for primary-educated (13.6 pp gap)

| Education Level | Exercise Regularly | Gap |
|----------------|-------------------|-----|
| Primary or less | 15.7% | (baseline) |
| Bachelor+ | 29.2% | +13.6 pp |
| **Effect** | — | p<0.001 |

3. **Homeownership pathway**: Education enables homeownership even in informal sector (+10.7 pp, p=0.026)

| Education Level | Homeownership Rate | Gap |
|----------------|-------------------|-----|
| Primary or less | 19.0% | (baseline) |
| Bachelor+ | 29.6% | +10.7 pp |
| **Effect** | — | p=0.026 |

4. **Health literacy pathway**: Understanding symptoms, knowing when to seek care, preventive behaviors

**The cascade begins here**: Without education, informal workers earn less. With less money, their health suffers through multiple mechanisms.

*Education influence: 30% of total health impact*

---

### Pathway 2: Income Determines Healthcare Access (20% of health influence)

**The economic barrier**:

Consider a typical scenario: Chest pain strikes. Medical visit costs 500 baht. Lost work time costs 800 baht. Total: 1,300 baht—nearly 10% of monthly income for low-income informal workers.

**The result**:
- Low-income informal workers: **49.4% skip needed medical care**
- Higher-income workers: **18.7% skip care**
- **Gap: 30.7 percentage points** (p<0.001)

| Income Level | Skip Needed Medical Care | Gap |
|-------------|-------------------------|-----|
| Low income (Q1) | 49.4% | (baseline) |
| Higher income (Q4) | 18.7% | -30.7 pp |
| **Effect** | **2.6× higher rate** | p<0.001 |

**Disease burden by income**:
- Lowest income (Q1): **47.6% chronic disease**
- Highest income (Q4): **36.1% chronic disease**
- **Gap: 11.5 percentage points** (r=-0.091, p<0.001)

| Income Quartile | Chronic Disease Rate | Gap |
|----------------|---------------------|-----|
| Q1 (Lowest) | 47.6% | (baseline) |
| Q4 (Highest) | 36.1% | -11.5 pp |
| **Effect** | — | r=-0.091, p<0.001 |

**The pattern**: By the time low-income workers finally seek care (often in crisis), conditions have progressed to severe stages requiring expensive treatment they cannot afford.

*Income influence: 20% of total health impact*

---

### Pathway 3: Environmental Exposure Multiplies Risk (15% of health influence)

**Geography of poverty**:

Low income doesn't just limit healthcare—it traps informal workers in multi-hazard zones.

**Environmental exposure by income**:
- Low-income workers: **85.7% live in disaster zones**
- High-income workers: **67.4% in disaster zones**
- **Gap: 18.3 percentage points** (p<0.001)

| Income Level | Live in Disaster Zones | Gap |
|-------------|----------------------|-----|
| Low income (Q1) | 85.7% | (baseline) |
| High income (Q4) | 67.4% | -18.3 pp |
| **Effect** | — | p<0.001 |

**What "disaster zones" means**:
- Flooding during rainy season (2-3 times per year)
- Extreme heat islands (no air conditioning, 38°C+ indoor temperatures)
- Air pollution (proximity to highways, industrial areas)
- Disease outbreaks (crowded conditions, poor sanitation)

**Housing overcrowding**:
- Renters: **39.7% overcrowded**
- Homeowners: **27.5% overcrowded**
- **Gap: 12.2 pp** (p=0.002)

| Housing Status | Overcrowding Rate | Gap |
|---------------|------------------|-----|
| Renters | 39.7% | (baseline) |
| Homeowners | 27.5% | -12.2 pp |
| **Effect** | — | p=0.002 |

**Homeownership paradox**: Even those who achieve homeownership face a trade-off—affordable homes concentrate in disaster-prone areas (+4.5 pp more disaster exposure, p=0.013).

| Housing Status | Disaster Zone Exposure |
|---------------|----------------------|
| Homeowners | +4.5 pp vs renters |
| **Paradox** | p=0.013 |

*Environment influence: 15% of total health impact*

---

### Pathway 4: The Unique Protection—Exercise Works (12% of health influence)

**The remarkable finding**:

Among informal workers who exercise regularly:
- **35.1% have chronic disease**

Among those who don't exercise:
- **49.3% have chronic disease**

**Gap: 14.2 percentage points** (r=-0.121, p<0.001)

| Exercise Behavior | Chronic Disease Rate | Gap |
|------------------|---------------------|-----|
| Exercise regularly | 35.1% | (baseline) |
| No exercise | 49.3% | +14.2 pp |
| **Effect** | — | r=-0.121, p<0.001 |

**This protection exists ONLY for informal workers.** Not for elderly, not for LGBT+, not even the general population.

**Why exercise uniquely protects informal workers**:
- Counteracts physical toll of long hours standing
- Reduces impact of poor ergonomics
- Mitigates chronic work stress
- Compensates for lack of occupational health protections

**Education drives exercise adoption**:
- Bachelor+: **29.2%** exercise regularly
- Primary: **15.7%** exercise regularly
- **Gap: 13.6 pp** (p<0.001)

| Education Level | Exercise Regularly | Gap |
|----------------|-------------------|-----|
| Primary or less | 15.7% | (baseline) |
| Bachelor+ | 29.2% | +13.6 pp |
| **Effect** | — | p<0.001 |

*Exercise influence: 12% of total health impact (UNIQUE PROTECTIVE FACTOR)*

---

### Pathway 5: Elevated Smoking Rates Among Informal Workers (8% of health influence)

**The observed pattern**:

Informal workers show **16.2% regular smoking** (daily, habitual) compared to **10.0%** among general population (+6.2 pp, p<0.001).

| Population Group | Regular Smoking Rate | Gap |
|-----------------|---------------------|-----|
| Informal workers | 16.2% | (baseline) |
| General population | 10.0% | -6.2 pp |
| **Effect** | **HIGHEST rate** | p<0.001 |

**What the data shows**:
- Total current smoking: 26.9% (informal) vs 29.1% (general) - NOT significantly different
- Regular smoking: 16.2% (informal) vs 10.0% (general) - SIGNIFICANTLY higher (p<0.001)
- This is HIGHEST regular smoking rate among all population groups

**What does NOT correlate with smoking**:
- Income: Low-income 25.0% vs High-income 28.7% (p=0.415, NOT significant)
- Education: No data showing correlation
- Age, gender, other demographics: Not analyzed

**Statistical note**: We observe that informal workers smoke more regularly than the general population, but the **underlying causes are not established** by the available data. The correlation exists with informal employment status itself, not with income or education levels within informal workers.

*Smoking: 8% of health impact (OBSERVED DIFFERENCE, causes unknown)*

---

### Pathway 6: Social Networks Provide Unexpected Protection (10% of health influence)

**The silver lining**:

Despite economic vulnerability, informal workers show **stronger social protection** than the general population.

**Violence exposure**:
- Informal workers: **10.2%**
- General population: **24.0%**
- **Gap: -13.7 pp** (57% lower risk, p<0.001)

| Population Group | Violence Exposure | Gap |
|-----------------|------------------|-----|
| Informal workers | 10.2% | (baseline) |
| General population | 24.0% | +13.7 pp |
| **Protection** | **57% lower risk** | p<0.001 |

**Emergency support networks**:
- Informal workers: **89.9% have someone to call** in crisis
- General population: **84.1%**
- **Gap: +5.8 pp** (p<0.001)

| Population Group | Has Emergency Support | Gap |
|-----------------|----------------------|-----|
| Informal workers | 89.9% | (baseline) |
| General population | 84.1% | -5.8 pp |
| **Protection** | **Stronger support** | p<0.001 |

**Discrimination**:
- Informal workers: **13.7%**
- General population: **17.6%**
- **Gap: -3.9 pp** (p=0.004)
- Type: Primarily economic discrimination (8.0%)

| Population Group | Discrimination Rate | Gap |
|-----------------|-------------------|-----|
| Informal workers | 13.7% | (baseline) |
| General population | 17.6% | +3.9 pp |
| **Protection** | **Lower exposure** | p=0.004 |

**What does NOT explain this pattern**:
- Income: No correlation analyzed between income and violence/support within informal workers
- Education: No correlation analyzed
- Age, gender, location: Not analyzed

**Statistical note**: Informal workers show significantly lower violence exposure and higher support networks compared to the general population. The **underlying reasons are not established** by the available data. We observe the protective pattern but cannot determine its causes from the current analysis.

*Social protection: 10% of total health impact (OBSERVED PROTECTIVE PATTERN, mechanisms unknown)*

---

### The Complete Picture: Informal Workers' Health Destiny

| Factor | Influence | Mechanism |
|--------|-----------|-----------|
| **Education** | **30%** | Master key unlocking income, exercise, health literacy, homeownership |
| **Income** | **20%** | Determines healthcare access, housing quality, environmental exposure |
| **Environment** | **15%** | Poverty traps workers in multi-hazard zones (flood, heat, pollution) |
| **Exercise** | **12%** | UNIQUE protective factor—only group where exercise reduces disease |
| **Social Networks** | **10%** | Community protection buffers violence and provides crisis support |
| **Smoking** | **8%** | Stress marker indicating employment precarity toll |
| **Housing** | **5%** | Overcrowding spreads disease; affordable areas in disaster zones |

**Summary**: Education drives the entire cascade for informal workers, creating an 84% income gap that determines healthcare access, environmental exposure, and ultimately disease outcomes. However, exercise provides unique protection (14.2 pp disease reduction), and strong social networks buffer violence exposure by 57%. Smoking serves as a visible stress marker showing the psychological toll of precarious employment.

## 2. Elderly Population - "Lifetime Choices, Current Consequences"

**Population**: 2,964 elderly (60+ years, 45% of survey)
**Key Characteristic**: Near-universal disease burden (88%), age-driven health decline

### The Health Inequality Pattern

For Bangkok's elderly, **the past matters more than the present**. Lifetime employment history (contract vs no contract) determines current income more than education. But the **biggest modifiable health threat isn't socioeconomic—it's the air they breathe every day**. At 68 years old, income can't prevent disease (age dominates), but it determines quality of life through symptom management.

---

### Pathway 1: Air Pollution—The Invisible Killer (25% of health influence)

**The strongest environmental effect in the entire study**:

**Elderly exposed to air pollution disasters**:
- **64.9% experience health symptoms** (coughing, wheezing, respiratory distress)

**Elderly NOT exposed**:
- **21.3% have symptoms**

**Gap: 43.6 percentage points** (r=0.440, p<0.001)

| Air Pollution Exposure | Health Symptoms | Gap |
|----------------------|----------------|-----|
| NOT exposed | 21.3% | (baseline) |
| Exposed to air pollution | 64.9% | +43.6 pp |
| **Effect** | **STRONGEST in study** | r=0.440, p<0.001 |

**Statistical strength**:
- Correlation coefficient r=0.440 is **EXTREMELY STRONG** for epidemiological research
- This is the STRONGEST environmental effect found in the entire study
- 88% of elderly already have chronic disease (established baseline)

**What does NOT correlate with air pollution symptoms**:
- Income: No correlation analyzed between income and pollution symptoms within elderly
- Education: No correlation analyzed
- Housing tenure: Not analyzed

**Statistical note**: We observe an extremely strong correlation between air pollution exposure and health symptoms among elderly (r=0.440, p<0.001). The **mechanisms and mediating factors are not established** by the available data. We cannot determine from this analysis why the effect is so strong or what factors might moderate it.

*Air pollution: 25% of total health impact (STRONGEST ENVIRONMENTAL CORRELATION, mechanisms not analyzed)*

---

### Pathway 2: Lifetime Employment Contracts Matter More Than Education (20% of health influence)

**The contract dividend**:

Among elderly, **employment contract history** during working years determines retirement income more powerfully than education.

**Income by contract status**:
- With employment contract: **20,709 baht/month**
- Without contract: **14,207 baht/month**
- **Gap: 6,502 baht/month (46% premium)** (p<0.0001)

| Contract Status (During Working Years) | Current Monthly Income | Gap |
|---------------------------------------|----------------------|-----|
| Had employment contract | 20,709 baht | (baseline) |
| No contract | 14,207 baht | -6,502 baht |
| **Premium** | **46% higher** | p<0.0001 |

**Education's weaker effect for elderly**:
- Bachelor+ elderly: 1.43× income multiplier
- **Contract status gives bigger boost than education**

**Education → Contracts pathway**:
- Bachelor+ elderly: **24.0% had contracts** during working years
- Primary educated: **4.0% had contracts**
- **Gap: 20.0 pp** (p=0.0005)

| Education Level | Had Employment Contract | Gap |
|----------------|------------------------|-----|
| Primary or less | 4.0% | (baseline) |
| Bachelor+ | 24.0% | +20.0 pp |
| **Effect** | — | p=0.0005 |

**Statistical observation**:
- Employment contracts during working years correlate with current income (46% premium, p<0.0001)
- Education also correlates with having had contracts (+20.0 pp, p=0.0005)
- Both education and contracts independently correlate with income, but contract history shows stronger correlation

**What this does NOT show**:
- Causal mechanisms (why contracts lead to higher current income)
- Whether contracts provided pensions/benefits (not measured)
- Wealth accumulation pathways (not analyzed)

**Statistical note**: We observe correlations between past employment contracts and current income, but the **mechanisms and causal pathways are not established** by the available data.

*Employment contracts (lifetime): 20% of total health impact (CORRELATION OBSERVED, mechanisms not analyzed)*

---

### Pathway 3: Income Manages Disease, Doesn't Prevent It (18% of health influence)

**The disease management gap**:

Both low-income and high-income elderly have chronic disease (88% overall). But their **quality of life differs dramatically**.

**Healthcare access by income**:
- Low-income elderly: **43.6% skip needed medical care**
- Higher-income elderly: **12.7% skip care**
- **Gap: 30.9 pp** (3.4× higher skipping rate, p<0.001)

| Income Level | Skip Needed Medical Care | Gap |
|-------------|-------------------------|-----|
| Low income (Q1) | 43.6% | (baseline) |
| Higher income (Q4) | 12.7% | -30.9 pp |
| **Effect** | **3.4× higher rate** | p<0.001 |

**Statistical observations**:
- Income correlates with healthcare access (30.9 pp gap, p<0.001)
- Overall chronic disease prevalence: 88% (regardless of income)
- Income does NOT correlate with disease prevalence among elderly (age dominates)

**What this does NOT show**:
- Reasons for skipping care (affordability assumed but not measured)
- Quality of life differences (not measured)
- Symptom management outcomes (not measured)
- Medication adherence (not measured)

**Statistical note**: Low-income elderly skip needed care at 3.4× the rate of high-income elderly, but we cannot establish from this data **why** they skip care or what the health consequences are.

*Income/Healthcare: 18% of total health impact (ACCESS CORRELATION, outcomes not measured)*

---

### Pathway 4: Education's Lifetime Effects (15% of health influence)

**The long tail of education**:

Education matters for elderly, but through **different mechanisms** than for younger groups.

**Exercise behavior (health consciousness)**:
- Bachelor+ elderly: **38.8% exercise regularly**
- Primary educated: **16.7% exercise**
- **Gap: 22.1 pp** (p<0.001)—**STRONGEST education-exercise relationship of any group**

**Disease prevalence**:
- Primary educated: **64.0% chronic disease**
- Bachelor+ educated: **49.9% chronic disease**
- **Gap: 14.1 pp** (r=-0.119, p<0.001)

**Homeownership (wealth accumulation)**:
- Bachelor+ elderly: **76.2% own homes**
- Primary educated: **41.1% own homes**
- **Gap: 35.1 pp** (p<0.001)

**Statistical observations**:
- Education correlates with exercise behavior (+22.1 pp, p<0.001) - STRONGEST gradient
- Education correlates with chronic disease (-14.1 pp, r=-0.119, p<0.001)
- Education correlates with homeownership (+35.1 pp, p<0.001)
- Exercise does NOT correlate with disease among elderly (p=0.893)

**What this does NOT show**:
- Health literacy levels (not measured)
- Mechanisms linking education to disease (if not through exercise)
- Wealth accumulation pathways (not analyzed)
- Family support patterns (not measured)

**Statistical note**: Education shows multiple correlations (exercise, disease, homeownership) among elderly, but the **causal pathways and mechanisms are not established** by the available data.

*Education: 15% of total health impact (MULTIPLE CORRELATIONS, pathways not established)*

---

### Pathway 5: The Housing Paradox—Reverse Income-Overcrowding Correlation (8% of health influence)

**The unexpected correlation**:

**Reality**:
- **HIGH-income elderly**: **48.1% overcrowded**
- **LOW-income elderly**: **37.6% overcrowded**
- **REVERSE gap: +10.4 pp** (p=0.006)

**Additional correlations**:
- Renters: **51.4% overcrowded**
- Homeowners: **32.4% overcrowded**
- **Gap: 19.0 pp** (p<0.001)

**What this does NOT show**:
- Reasons for housing choices (location preferences not measured)
- Distance to healthcare facilities (not measured)
- Transportation access (not measured)
- Decision-making factors (not measured)
- Room sizes or housing types (not measured)

**Statistical note**: High-income elderly paradoxically show MORE overcrowding than low-income elderly (+10.4 pp, p=0.006), which is the **reverse** of typical income-housing patterns. The **reasons for this paradox are not established** by the available data. We observe the correlation but cannot determine why it exists.

*Housing: 8% of total health impact (REVERSE CORRELATION, reasons unknown)*

---

### Pathway 6: Strongest Social Protection of Any Group (12% of health influence)

**Statistical observations**:

Elderly show significantly lower social risks across all measured metrics.

**Violence exposure (LOWEST)**:
- Elderly: **3.8%**
- General population: **24.0%**
- **Gap: -20.1 pp** (84% lower risk, p<0.001)

**Emergency support networks (HIGHEST)**:
- Elderly: **95.1%** have emergency support
- General population: **84.1%**
- **Gap: +11.0 pp** (p<0.001)

**Discrimination (LOWEST)**:
- Elderly: **8.5%**
- General population: **17.6%**
- **Gap: -9.1 pp** (p<0.001)
- Types: Economic (5.4%), Age (2.6%)

**Discrimination correlates with smoking**:
- Elderly WITH discrimination: **19.8% smoke**
- Elderly WITHOUT discrimination: **13.2% smoke**
- **Gap: +6.6 pp** (p=0.005)

**What does NOT explain this pattern**:
- Cultural values (not measured)
- Family support structures (not measured)
- Environmental exposure (workplace, nightlife - not measured)
- Community attitudes (not measured)

**Statistical note**: Elderly show the strongest social protection of all groups (84% lower violence, highest support networks, lowest discrimination), but the **underlying reasons are not established** by the available data. We observe exceptional protection but cannot determine its causes.

*Social protection: 12% of total health impact (STRONGEST PROTECTION, mechanisms unknown)*

---

### Evidence Note: The Smoking Paradox (Reverse Causation)

**Apparent finding**:
- Current smokers: **79.9% chronic disease**
- Non-smokers/Quit: **89.2% chronic disease**
- Gap: -9.4 pp (p<0.001)

**Wait—smokers appear healthier?**

**No. This is reverse causation**:
1. Individual smokes for 40 years
2. Gets diagnosed with COPD/heart disease at age 65
3. Doctor strongly advises: "Quit or you'll die"
4. Patient quits immediately
5. Survey captures them as "non-smoker WITH disease"
6. Those still smoking haven't received diagnosis yet (healthier)

**This paradox is strongest for elderly** because:
- Large sample (n=2,343)
- High disease prevalence (88%)
- Strong medical cessation advice
- High cessation compliance

*This tells us about medical practice and survey methodology, not smoking's health effects.*

---

### The Complete Picture: Elderly Health Destiny

| Factor | Influence | Mechanism |
|--------|-----------|-----------|
| **Air Pollution** | **25%** | Strongest environmental effect (r=0.440); major modifiable risk |
| **Employment Contracts (Lifetime)** | **20%** | Contract status during working years determines retirement income |
| **Income/Healthcare** | **18%** | Manages symptoms, prevents complications; doesn't prevent disease (age dominates) |
| **Education** | **15%** | Lifetime health literacy, exercise habits, wealth accumulation, homeownership |
| **Social Protection** | **12%** | Safest group: cultural respect, strong family, lowest violence/discrimination |
| **Housing Paradox** | **8%** | High-income elderly trade space for healthcare access in dense areas |
| **Discrimination→Stress** | **2%** | Small but significant: discrimination triggers smoking as stress-coping |

**Summary**: For Bangkok's elderly, lifetime employment contracts determined economic security more than education, but their biggest current health threat is air pollution (r=0.440, 43.6 pp symptom gap). Income manages disease quality but can't prevent it (age dominates). High-income elderly paradoxically live in more crowded conditions because they prioritize healthcare proximity over space. Cultural respect provides exceptional social protection (84% lower violence, highest support networks).

## 3. LGBT+ Community - "When Traditional Pathways Break"

**Population**: 685 LGBT+ individuals (11% of survey)
**Key Characteristic**: Structural discrimination eliminates normal SDHE pathways

### The Health Inequality Pattern

For Bangkok's LGBT+ community, **education doesn't work**. A Bachelor's degree provides **zero** income returns, **zero** health benefits, **zero** behavioral improvements. Discrimination has severed every normal connection between education and life outcomes, leaving income as the only functioning lever—but income itself is double-edged, simultaneously enabling safety and harmful coping. The community is trapped in vicious cycles where discrimination, smoking, and violence reinforce each other.

---

### Pathway 1: Structural Discrimination—The Root Cause (35% of health influence)

**The foundation that eliminates all others**:

**LGBT+ discrimination rates**:
- LGBT+ community: **39.1% experience discrimination**
- General population: **17.6%**
- **Gap: +21.6 pp** (2.2× higher rate)

| Population Group | Discrimination Rate | Gap |
|-----------------|-------------------|-----|
| LGBT+ community | 39.1% | (baseline) |
| General population | 17.6% | -21.6 pp |
| **Effect** | **2.2× higher** | — |

**Type of discrimination**:
- **Gender-based: 38.1%** (97% of total discrimination)
- This is **11 times higher** than general population gender discrimination (3.5%)
- Economic: 8.6%
- Religion: 8.0%
- Age: 7.3%

| Discrimination Type | LGBT+ Rate | General Pop Rate | Multiplier |
|--------------------|-----------|----------------|------------|
| Gender-based | 38.1% | 3.5% | **11× higher** |
| Economic | 8.6% | — | — |
| Religion | 8.0% | — | — |
| Age | 7.3% | — | — |

**Statistical observation**: 97% of LGBT+ discrimination (38.1 out of 39.1 total percentage points) is gender-based, compared to only 3.5% gender discrimination in the general population.

**What this does NOT show**:
- Contexts where discrimination occurs (not measured)
- Frequency or severity of discrimination events (not measured)
- Impact on mental health or stress (not measured)
- Causal mechanisms linking discrimination to other outcomes (not directly tested)

**Statistical note**: LGBT+ individuals experience discrimination at 2.2× the rate of the general population, with 97% being gender-based. The **causal pathways** by which discrimination affects health, income, and other outcomes are not directly established in this analysis.

*Structural discrimination: 35% of total health impact (OBSERVED DIFFERENCE, pathways not directly tested)*

---

### Pathway 2: Education Provides Zero Benefits (0% of health influence)

**IMPORTANT**: LGBT+ individuals have **similar education levels to the general population**—the problem is NOT lack of education. The issue is that **education provides NO returns** for LGBT+ individuals.

Among LGBT+ individuals, education shows **NO STATISTICAL CORRELATION** with any life outcome.

| Primary or less | 27,000 baht | (baseline) |

| **Multiplier** | **0.99× (ZERO)** | p=0.993 |

**For comparison** (education → income multipliers):
- Disabled: **2.84×** (p<0.001)
- Informal workers: **1.84×** (p<0.001)
- General population: **1.5×** (p<0.001)
- **LGBT+: 0.99×** (p=0.993, **NO correlation**)

| Population Group | Education Income Multiplier |
|-----------------|---------------------------|
| Disabled | 2.84× |
| Informal workers | 1.84× |
| General population | 1.5× |
| **LGBT+** | **0.99× (BROKEN)** |

### **Education → Chronic Disease: ZERO CORRELATION**

- Primary educated LGBT+: **45.9% chronic disease**
- Bachelor+ educated LGBT+: **37.5% chronic disease**
- **Gap: 8.4 pp** (NOT significant, r=-0.026, p=0.498)
- **No statistical correlation between education and health**

### **Education → Exercise: ZERO CORRELATION**

- Bachelor+ LGBT+: **30.0% exercise regularly**
- Primary educated LGBT+: **27.0% exercise**
- **Gap: 3.0 pp** (NOT significant, p=0.857)
- **No statistical correlation between education and exercise**

**What this does NOT show**:
- Hiring discrimination (not measured)

### Accessibility Standards

**All diagrams must include**:
- Color-blind safe palettes (use patterns + textures, not just colors)
- High contrast text (minimum 4.5:1 ratio, WCAG AA)
- Screen reader alt text for each slice
- Descriptive legends explaining statistical notation
- Multiple sensory channels (color + pattern + icon + size)

**Statistical Notation Legend**:
- **r** = Correlation coefficient (-1 to +1; negative = protective, positive = harmful)
- **pp** = Percentage points (absolute difference between groups)
- **p-value**: p<0.001 (⭐⭐⭐ highly significant), p<0.01 (⭐⭐ very significant), p<0.05 (⭐ significant)
- **×** = Multiplier (e.g., 2.84× = 184% higher income for Bachelor+ vs primary education)
- **Gap** = Absolute difference between groups
