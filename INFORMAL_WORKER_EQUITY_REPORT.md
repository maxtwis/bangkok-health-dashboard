## Part 1: Informal vs Formal Workers Comparison (Context)


### Monthly income (baht)

**Informal:** 16,363 baht (n=1,381)
**Formal:** 28,670 baht (n=1,179)
**Gap:** -12,306 baht (-43%)
**Statistical Test:** p < 0.001

**Interpretation:**
Informal workers earn 43% less than formal workers, translating to a monthly income gap of 12,306 baht. This represents a **147,672 baht annual income penalty** for informal work. The median informal worker earns approximately 16,000 baht/month, far below Bangkok's living wage for a family. This income disparity reflects the fundamental structural disadvantage of informal employment: no minimum wage protection, no salary progression, and vulnerability to economic shocks.

**Policy Implication:**
- Implement a **minimum income guarantee** of 15,000 baht/month for informal workers through a combination of wage subsidies and social protection
- Create sector-specific minimum rates for common informal occupations (e.g., street vending licenses tied to minimum daily income targets)
- Establish informal worker cooperatives to increase bargaining power and income stability


### Has employment contract

**Informal:** 4.2% (n=1,853)
**Formal:** 84.9% (n=1,430)
**Gap:** -80.7 pp
**Statistical Test:** p < 0.001

**Interpretation:**
Only **1 in 25 informal workers** has an employment contract, compared to **17 in 20 formal workers**. This 80.7 percentage point gap reveals the complete absence of legal employment protections for informal workers. Without contracts, informal workers have no recourse for unfair dismissal, no protection from exploitation, and no legal basis for claiming workplace rights. This makes them invisible to labor law and vulnerable to abusive working conditions.

**Policy Implication:**
- Create a **simplified contract template** for informal workers (1-page, Thai language, standardized terms) to formalize common arrangements like domestic work, delivery services, and construction
- Offer tax incentives for employers who contract informal workers (e.g., 50% labor tax reduction for first year)
- Establish mobile labor rights clinics in informal work zones to help workers understand and assert their rights


### Has work benefits

**Informal:** 3.8% (n=1,853)
**Formal:** 87.9% (n=1,430)
**Gap:** -84.1 pp
**Statistical Test:** p < 0.001

**Interpretation:**
Only **1 in 26 informal workers** receives work benefits (health insurance, paid leave, bonuses), compared to **9 in 10 formal workers**. This 84.1 percentage point gap means informal workers face the full cost of sickness, accidents, and emergencies without employer support. A single illness can push an informal worker into debt, as they lose both income (no sick leave) and incur medical costs (no health insurance). This creates a poverty trap where health shocks become financial catastrophes.

**Policy Implication:**
- Extend mandatory employer contributions to Social Security Fund to include informal workers (prorated based on income)
- Create a **universal basic benefits package** funded by general taxation: 30 days paid sick leave, accident insurance, and death/disability benefits
- Pilot **portable benefits systems** where benefits follow the worker across multiple informal jobs


### Work injury (serious, past 12 months)

**Informal:** 5.6% (n=1,853)
**Formal:** 8.0% (n=1,430)
**Gap:** -2.4 pp
**Statistical Test:** p = 0.007

**Interpretation:**
Informal workers have slightly **lower serious injury rates** than formal workers (5.6% vs 8.0%). This 2.4 percentage point difference is statistically significant but counterintuitive. The finding likely reflects **under-reporting bias** rather than safer conditions: informal workers may not classify injuries as "serious" if they cannot afford to stop working, or they may work in sectors with different injury types (e.g., street vendors vs factory workers). Alternatively, formal workers in manufacturing and construction may face higher exposure to machinery-related injuries.

**Policy Implication:**
- Investigate injury patterns by occupation type to understand true risk exposure for informal workers
- Extend workplace safety inspections to informal work sites (markets, construction sites, home-based work)
- Create injury reporting incentives (e.g., free medical treatment for reported work injuries regardless of employment status)


### Work injury (minor, past 12 months)

**Informal:** 14.4% (n=1,853)
**Formal:** 14.3% (n=1,430)
**Gap:** +0.1 pp
**Statistical Test:** p = 0.993 (not significant)

**Interpretation:**
Informal and formal workers experience **identical minor injury rates** (14.4% vs 14.3%). This finding suggests that occupational hazards exist across all work types, regardless of employment status. Minor injuries (cuts, bruises, sprains) are common in both blue-collar informal work (construction, delivery) and formal work (manufacturing, warehousing). The lack of difference indicates that injury prevention needs to target work activities rather than employment status.

**Policy Implication:**
- Implement universal workplace safety training programs accessible to all workers (online modules, community workshops)
- Distribute free personal protective equipment (PPE) through district health centers for high-risk occupations
- Create peer-to-peer safety networks where experienced workers train newcomers in injury prevention


### Skip medical care due to cost

**Informal:** 13.3% (n=1,853)
**Formal:** 18.7% (n=1,430)
**Gap:** -5.4 pp
**Statistical Test:** p < 0.001

**Interpretation:**
Surprisingly, informal workers are **less likely to skip medical care** than formal workers (13.3% vs 18.7%). This 5.4 percentage point gap is unexpected given informal workers' lower income and lack of health benefits. Possible explanations: (1) informal workers have more flexible schedules to visit free public clinics during weekday hours; (2) informal workers are older and more likely to be enrolled in universal coverage schemes; (3) formal workers face time constraints from rigid work schedules that prevent clinic visits. This finding challenges the assumption that informal work always means worse health access.

**Policy Implication:**
- Extend public clinic hours to evenings and weekends to accommodate formal workers' schedules
- Investigate why formal workers skip care despite higher incomes - address time poverty and employer leave policies
- Ensure universal health coverage enrollment is maintained for workers transitioning between informal and formal employment


### Food insecurity (reduced meals)

**Informal:** 5.1% (n=1,853)
**Formal:** 13.6% (n=1,430)
**Gap:** -8.6 pp
**Statistical Test:** p < 0.001

**Interpretation:**
Informal workers report **significantly lower food insecurity** than formal workers (5.1% vs 13.6%). This 8.6 percentage point gap is another counterintuitive finding. Possible explanations: (1) informal workers are more likely to live in multi-generational households with shared food resources; (2) informal workers have more control over work schedules to prepare home-cooked meals (cheaper than buying food); (3) age composition differences - older informal workers may receive family support or pensions; (4) formal workers may face higher fixed costs (rent, transport) that squeeze food budgets. This finding suggests household structure and time flexibility matter more than income alone for food security.

**Policy Implication:**
- Investigate food insecurity drivers among formal workers - target interventions at high-cost-of-living groups (renters, single-person households)
- Expand workplace cafeteria subsidies for formal workers in low-wage sectors
- Create community food programs accessible to all workers regardless of employment status (e.g., subsidized community kitchens)


### Working hours per day

**Informal:** 8 hours (n=1,843)
**Formal:** 9 hours (n=1,283)
**Gap:** -1 hour (-8%)
**Statistical Test:** p < 0.001

**Interpretation:**
Informal workers work **1 hour less per day** than formal workers (8 vs 9 hours). This 8% difference reflects the trade-off informal workers make: **lower income for greater time flexibility**. Informal workers can set their own schedules, adjust work hours based on demand, and balance work with caregiving or health needs. However, shorter hours also mean lower daily earnings, contributing to the 43% income gap. The finding reveals that informal work offers autonomy but not prosperity.

**Policy Implication:**
- Recognize time flexibility as a valued aspect of informal work - preserve worker autonomy in formalization efforts
- Create hybrid employment models that combine formal protections (contracts, benefits) with flexible scheduling
- Pilot hourly-wage schemes for informal workers to ensure fair compensation for actual hours worked

## Domain 1: Healthcare Access


### Finding 1.1: Skip medical care due to cost by Income Level

**Statistical Test:** Chi-square = 105.4, p < 0.001, Cramer's V = 0.28 (small-to-medium effect)

**Results:**

| Income Group | Skip Medical Rate | Sample Size |
|--------------|-------------------|-------------|
| **Low (<10,500 baht)** | **32.9%** (116 out of 353) | 353 |
| **Middle (10,500-15,000 baht)** | **12.7%** (77 out of 605) | 605 |
| **High (>15,000 baht)** | **6.9%** (29 out of 423) | 423 |

**Equity Gap:** 26.0 percentage points

**Interpretation:**
Low-income informal workers are **4.8 times more likely** to skip medical care than high-income informal workers (32.9% vs 6.9%). This means **1 in 3 low-income informal workers** skip needed medical care due to cost, despite Thailand's universal health coverage. The 26 percentage point gap reveals that universal coverage is not reaching the poorest informal workers. Barriers likely include: (1) out-of-pocket costs for medications and diagnostics not covered by the 30-baht scheme; (2) transportation costs to reach hospitals; (3) opportunity cost of lost work hours during clinic visits; (4) informal payments or under-the-table fees. The internal benchmark of 6.9% (high-income rate) shows that even among informal workers, adequate income nearly eliminates cost-based medical skipping.

**Policy Implication:**
1. **Income-targeted healthcare subsidies**: Provide 500 baht/month healthcare credit cards to informal workers earning <10,000 baht/month, usable for medications, transport to clinics, and medical supplies
2. **Mobile health clinics in informal work zones**: Deploy weekly mobile clinics to markets, construction sites, and residential areas where low-income informal workers are concentrated, eliminating transportation barriers
3. **Zero out-of-pocket costs for essential services**: Expand the 30-baht scheme to fully cover common chronic disease medications (hypertension, diabetes), diagnostic tests, and preventive care for low-income informal workers
4. **Income replacement during medical visits**: Pilot cash transfers (200 baht per clinic visit) to compensate for lost work hours, reducing opportunity costs


### Finding 1.2: Skip medical care due to cost by Disability Status

**Statistical Test:** Chi-square = 10.3, p = 0.001, Cramer's V = 0.075 (negligible effect)

**Results:**

| Disability Status | Skip Medical Rate | Sample Size |
|-------------------|-------------------|-------------|
| **Has disability** | **27.3%** (18 out of 66) | 66 |
| **No disability** | **12.8%** (229 out of 1,787) | 1,787 |

**Equity Gap:** 14.5 percentage points

**Interpretation:**
Informal workers with disabilities are **2.1 times more likely** to skip medical care than non-disabled informal workers (27.3% vs 12.8%). More than **1 in 4 disabled informal workers** forgoes needed medical care due to cost. This gap reflects compounded vulnerability: disabilities increase medical needs while simultaneously reducing earning capacity (as shown in Finding 3.13). Disabled informal workers face a vicious cycle where disability limits income, which prevents healthcare access, which worsens health and further limits work capacity. Transportation barriers are particularly severe for disabled workers navigating Bangkok's inaccessible public transport and healthcare facilities.

**Policy Implication:**
1. **Disability healthcare allowance**: Provide 1,000 baht/month healthcare allowance to disabled informal workers, recognizing their higher medical needs and lower earning capacity
2. **Accessible mobile health services**: Deploy mobile clinics with wheelchair accessibility and disability-trained staff to reach disabled informal workers at home or workplace
3. **Free medical transportation**: Create a dial-a-ride service for disabled informal workers to access healthcare facilities (similar to Bangkok's existing disabled transport programs but expanded)
4. **Integrated disability support**: Link healthcare access to income support programs to address the root cause - ensure disabled informal workers receive both medical care and economic assistance


### Finding 1.3: Skip medical care due to cost by Has Children (<5)

**Statistical Test:** Chi-square = 7.4, p = 0.006, Cramer's V = 0.063 (negligible effect)

**Results:**

| Has Children (<5) | Skip Medical Rate | Sample Size |
|-------------------|-------------------|-------------|
| **Yes** | **21.5%** (37 out of 172) | 172 |
| **No** | **13.8%** (232 out of 1,681) | 1,681 |

**Equity Gap:** 7.7 percentage points

**Interpretation:**
Informal workers with young children (<5 years old) are **1.6 times more likely** to skip medical care than those without young children (21.5% vs 13.8%). More than **1 in 5 informal workers with young children** skip their own medical care due to cost. This finding reveals **parental sacrifice**: when household budgets are tight, informal workers prioritize children's healthcare over their own. Young children require frequent medical visits (vaccinations, illness), consuming both money and time. Informal workers with young children also face higher childcare costs and reduced work hours, creating a double squeeze on household finances. The 7.7 percentage point gap shows that having young children creates a hidden health access penalty.

**Policy Implication:**
1. **Family healthcare packages**: Provide bundled healthcare coverage for informal workers with young children that includes both parent and child services, preventing parents from skipping their own care
2. **Childcare during medical visits**: Offer free childcare at district health centers so informal workers can attend appointments without leaving children unattended or paying for childcare
3. **Home visit programs**: Deploy community health workers to visit informal worker households with young children, providing preventive care at home and reducing need for costly clinic visits
4. **Parental health incentives**: Provide cash incentives (200 baht) for informal workers with young children who complete annual health checkups, encouraging preventive care

More than **half of all renter informal workers** suffer from oral health issues. 2. **Workplace dental screening**: Deploy mobile dental clinics to informal work zones for free annual screenings and preventive care (reducing need for expensive treatments later)
3. **Dental hygiene education**: Distribute free toothbrushes/toothpaste through community centers and provide oral health education to prevent problems
4. **Rent-to-income subsidies**: Reduce housing cost burdens for informal workers through rent subsidies, freeing up income for healthcare including dental care

### Finding 1.6: Personal health expenses (baht/month) by Home Ownership

**Statistical Test:** ANOVA = 4.3, p = 0.014, Eta-squared = 0.005 (negligible effect)

**Results:**

|----------------|------|--------|-------------|

| **Other** | **402 baht** | 100 baht | 537 |
| **Renter** | **376 baht** | 200 baht | 723 |

**Equity Gap:** 155 baht

**Interpretation:**
Homeowner informal workers spend **155 baht more per month** on healthcare than renters (531 vs 376 baht). This gap is **counterintuitive** - renters are poorer but spend less on healthcare. The finding likely reflects **ability to pay** rather than medical needs: homeowners can afford to spend more on healthcare (medications, supplements, preventive care), while renters ration healthcare spending due to tight budgets. The low median values (100-229 baht) compared to means (376-531 baht) indicate right-skewed distributions - a few people spend large amounts while most spend very little. Renters likely delay care and only spend when absolutely necessary.

**Policy Implication:**
1. **Investigate unmet health needs among renters**: Survey renters to identify what healthcare services they need but cannot afford (gap between actual spending and needed spending)
2. **Preventive care incentives for renters**: Provide free preventive care packages (annual checkups, chronic disease screening) specifically for low-income renters to catch problems early before they become expensive
3. **Healthcare spending vouchers**: Give monthly healthcare vouchers (300 baht) to renters earning <15,000 baht/month to increase their healthcare purchasing power
4. **Financial counseling**: Help informal workers budget for healthcare expenses and access available subsidies to prevent care rationing

3. **Healthcare-included rent**: Pilot social housing programs where rent includes healthcare coverage (bundled rent+health packages)

## Domain 2: Chronic Diseases & Disability


### Finding 2.1: Has chronic diseases by Age Group

**Statistical Test:** Chi-square = 282.6, p < 0.001, Cramer's V = 0.39 (medium effect)

**Results:**

| Age Group | Chronic Disease Rate | Sample Size |
|-----------|---------------------|-------------|
| **60+** | **71.8%** (606 out of 844) | 844 |
| **45-59** | **47.8%** (271 out of 567) | 567 |
| **30-44** | **26.0%** (82 out of 315) | 315 |
| **15-29** | **16.9%** (21 out of 124) | 124 |

**Equity Gap:** 54.9 percentage points

**Interpretation:**
Elderly informal workers (60+) have a **chronic disease rate 4.2 times higher** than youth (71.8% vs 16.9%). This 54.9 percentage point gap is the **largest health equity gap** in the entire report. More than **7 in 10 elderly informal workers** live with chronic disease (hypertension, diabetes, heart disease), compared to just **1 in 6 youth**. The gradient is steep - each age decade adds approximately 15-25 percentage points of disease prevalence. This finding reveals that informal work offers no retirement safety net: elderly informal workers continue working despite severe disease burdens because they cannot afford to stop. The finding also shows that chronic disease is nearly universal among elderly informal workers, making disease management (not prevention) the priority policy target.

**Policy Implication:**
1. **Universal chronic disease care for elderly informal workers**: Provide free chronic disease medications (hypertension, diabetes) to all informal workers age 60+, regardless of income, recognizing the 72% prevalence rate
2. **Elderly informal worker pensions**: Create a basic pension (3,000 baht/month) for informal workers age 65+ to enable retirement and reduce need to work despite chronic illness
3. **Integrated disease management programs**: Deploy community health workers to provide home-based chronic disease monitoring and medication delivery to elderly informal workers, reducing clinic visit burdens
4. **Age-based work modifications**: Create light-duty informal work opportunities (e.g., stationary vending, home-based work) suitable for elderly workers with chronic diseases who cannot perform physically demanding tasks

Low-education informal workers have a **chronic disease rate 1.6 times higher** than high-education workers (65.6% vs 40.0%). Nearly **2 in 3 low-education informal workers** live with chronic disease. This 25.6 percentage point gap reflects the social determinants of health: low-education informal workers likely have poor health literacy (less knowledge about disease prevention), higher-stress lifestyles, limited access to preventive care, and longer lifetime exposure to unhealthy working conditions. 1. **Health literacy programs**: Provide chronic disease prevention education tailored to low-literacy populations (visual materials, peer educators, hands-on demonstrations) focusing on diet, exercise, and early warning signs
2. **Free chronic disease screening**: Conduct annual screening campaigns in low-education communities to catch hypertension and diabetes early, before complications develop
3. **Simplified disease management**: Create easy-to-follow medication regimens with pictorial instructions for low-education informal workers managing chronic conditions
4. **Address root causes**: Expand adult education programs to break the link between low education and poor health in the next generation

Homeowner informal workers have **14.0 percentage points higher chronic disease rates** than renters (62.4% vs 48.4%). This gap is **counterintuitive** - homeowners are wealthier but sicker. Low-income workers earning 7,755 baht/month (see Finding 3.1) struggle to afford adequate food after paying for housing, transport, and utilities. The gradient across income groups (7.1%  4.0%  2.7%) is less steep than for extreme food insecurity (Finding 3.9), suggesting that **moderate food insecurity** affects workers across income levels, while extreme deprivation is concentrated among the poorest.

**Policy Implication:**
1. **Income-targeted food subsidies**: Provide food vouchers specifically to informal workers earning <10,000 baht/month to close the 4.4 percentage point gap
2. **Raise minimum income**: Increase income support to ensure all informal workers earn enough to afford adequate food (minimum 12,000 baht/month)
3. **Subsidized food prices**: Create price controls or subsidies for basic food items (rice, eggs, vegetables) accessible to low-income informal workers
4. **Monitor food insecurity**: Establish regular food insecurity screening for low-income informal workers to track progress and identify those needing assistance

## Conclusion

This report documents **81 significant equity gaps** within Bangkok's informal worker population, providing statistical evidence for targeted policy interventions. The analysis reveals that informal work is not a homogeneous category - massive inequities exist by income, age, education, disability, housing status, and family composition.

Key findings show that low-income informal workers face a **quadruple burden**: they skip medical care at 5x the rate of high-income workers, experience chronic disease at higher rates, earn 259% less, and face severe food insecurity. Disabled informal workers experience compounded disadvantage - earning 19% less while facing 2.1x higher healthcare skipping rates. Informal workers with young children sacrifice their own healthcare and food security to meet children's needs.

However, the findings also reveal **achievable targets** through internal benchmarks: high-income informal workers achieve 6.9% medical skipping rates (vs 32.9% for low-income), demonstrating that adequate income nearly eliminates cost-based healthcare barriers even within informal work.

These findings provide an evidence base for **equity-focused policy interventions** targeting the most disadvantaged informal workers: income support for low earners, comprehensive family packages for parents with young children, elderly pensions, disability allowances, rent subsidies, and free chronic disease care.
